## Overview

This paper introduces Spindt cathode field emission array technology developed at SRI International and surveys its potential space applications. Spindt cathodes are microfabricated cold cathode devices that emit electrons via field emission, offering dramatic advantages over thermionic emitters and plasma contactors in terms of power, mass, and efficiency. The paper gives particular attention to their use in electrodynamic tether systems.

## What Are Spindt Cathodes?

Spindt cathodes, first developed in the late 1960s, consist of an insulating layer (SiO₂) sandwiched between two conductors — a lower base and an upper gate electrode. An array of holes through the gate and insulator contains sharp metal emitter tips fabricated via thin film deposition. Emission is controlled by adjusting the gate-to-tip voltage, typically below 100 V. The structure is roughly ¾ µm in scale.

Key performance figures include packing densities exceeding 5 × 10⁷ tips/cm², per-tip emission up to 100 µA, and a theoretical current capacity of 5000 A/cm² for dense arrays. Emission follows the Fowler-Nordheim equation, I = (na)V²e^(−b/V), where *n* is the number of tips, *a* relates to emitting area and geometry, and *b* is proportional to the work function. Fowler-Nordheim plots of experimental data allow extraction of these coefficients for design extrapolation.

For a modeled 5-million-tip array (easily manufacturable at the time of writing), 0.1 A could be achieved at ~60 V gate voltage and 1 A at ~70 V.

## Advantages for Space

Spindt cathodes possess a suite of properties that make them well-suited to the space environment:

- **Low power and mass** due to microfabricated construction
- **No expendables** required (unlike plasma contactors that consume gas)
- **Clean operation** without the outgassing and contamination of hot cathodes
- **Wide temperature range**, from approximately −270 °C to over 400 °C
- **Long lifetime**, with tests demonstrating continuous operation up to eight years (terminated by facility power failure, not device failure)
- **Rapid switching** capability, operating in the microwave frequency regime
- **Self-powered mode** possible when a spacecraft already has a large negative bias relative to the ambient plasma — the gate is biased slightly positive relative to the tips, and the spacecraft-to-plasma potential drives electron extraction, consuming only the small fraction of current intercepted by the gate

## Space Applications

**Electric propulsion:** Spindt cathodes can serve as compact, efficient ionizers for plasma propulsion systems, free of hot cathode contaminants. They had already been applied in ground-based sample ionization and flown aboard a comet Halley flyby mission as a mass spectrometer ionization source.

**Spacecraft charge control and measurement:** Field emitters naturally address negative charging (common in both LEO and GEO) by emitting electrons with no expendables. A self-powered charge control system was proposed as early as 1993.

**Electron beam generation:** Spindt cathodes substitute for thermionic sources, offering rapid switching, high currents, and small gating voltages.

**Electrodynamic tethers:** A conducting tether moving through Earth's magnetic field generates an EMF that can drive current if electrical contact with the surrounding plasma is established at both ends. At the negative end (electron-emitting end), Spindt cathodes provide efficient emission with minimal potential drop, maximizing tether current and thus the electrodynamic force available for orbit raising, lowering, or de-orbiting — all without propellant.

## Remaining Engineering Challenges

The authors identified several areas needing further development: optimizing materials and coatings to withstand the chemically reactive space environment, ensuring contamination-free handling and packaging before deployment, managing slow initial power-on procedures to prevent arcing, addressing space-charge-limited current escape at high current densities or low plasma densities, and completing space qualification testing. None of these were considered fundamental obstacles.

## Conclusion

The paper positions Spindt cathodes as a transformative technology for space electron emission, arguing they are ready for application and offer clear advantages over thermionic emitters and plasma contactors across a range of missions — from charge control to electric propulsion to electrodynamic tether operations.
